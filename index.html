THE MOON

is Earth’s only natural satellite and the fifth-largest moon in our solar system. It orbits Earth at an average distance of about 238,855 miles (384,400 kilometers), taking roughly 27.3 days to complete one revolution. The Moon does not produce its own light; instead, it shines by reflecting sunlight, which makes it visible in the night sky. Its surface is covered in craters formed by asteroid and meteorite impacts over billions of years, along with vast plains of solidified lava known as maria.

ABOUT THE MOON

The Moon has no atmosphere, so there is no weather, wind, or sound, and temperatures can swing dramatically—from about +200°C during the day to -200°C at night. Due to tidal locking, the same side of the Moon always faces Earth, though we can see about 60% of its surface over time. The Moon plays a crucial role in stabilizing Earth’s axial tilt and creating ocean tides through its gravitational pull. Humans first landed on the Moon in 1969 during NASA’s Apollo 11 mission, when astronauts Neil Armstrong and Buzz Aldrin became the first people to walk on its surface. Since then, the Moon has remained a key focus for scientific exploration and future space colonization efforts.
